Doable and Undoable: A Comprehensive Review and Discussion on the Boundary of Graph Neural Networks

Abstract


Graph query processing has long been a central topic in the field of data science, holding a pivotal position within the database and data mining communities. Traditional approaches have focused on developing efficient, algorithm-based solutions, such as Dijkstra's algorithm for shortest paths, the Louvain method for community detection, and greedy algorithms for influence maximization. These methods typically come with solid theoretical guarantees and are optimized for specific, well-defined structural properties of graphs.

Concurrently, the machine learning community, particularly in the deep learning domain, has given rise to Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), a powerful tool whose core strength lies in its exceptional representation learning capabilities. Through a message-passing mechanism, GNNs can learn low-dimensional vector representations (embeddings) that fuse node attributes with complex local topological structures in an end-to-end fashion. As these two relatively independent fields begin to deeply intersect, a critical knowledge gap has emerged: there is currently no systematic framework to clearly delineate the application boundaries of GNNs for traditional graph query problems.

This survey aims to fill this gap. We propose a novel and principled classification framework that divides graph query problems into three distinct categories based on the functional role of GNNs: (1) problems that can fully solved end-to-end by GNNs; (2) problems where GNNs serve as an enhanced component within an existing algorithmic framework; and (3) problems where GNNs are entirely unsuitable for direct solutions and can only play an auxiliary role. By systematically clarifying these boundaries, this work provides a structured analytical framework that runs through the entire paper, offering meaningful guidance for the future application of GNNs in graph query processing.
